How to Answer "Why Do You Want to Work Here?"

RoleCall Blog · June 2, 2026 · 8 min read

It sounds simple. Then you're sitting across from a hiring manager, and the question lands: "Why do you want to work here?" Your brain cycles through a dozen half-formed thoughts. You want to say something genuine, something impressive, something that doesn't sound like every other candidate. But most people freeze, default to flattery, or ramble about themselves.

This question is one of the most common interview prompts across every industry and every role. It's also one of the most poorly answered. The good news: with a clear framework and some targeted research, you can turn this from a stumbling block into one of the strongest moments in your interview.

Here's exactly how to do it.

Why Interviewers Actually Ask This Question

Before you craft your answer, you need to understand what the interviewer is really evaluating. They are not asking you to compliment the company. They are not testing whether you memorized the "About Us" page. They are trying to figure out three things:

  1. Did you do your homework? A candidate who can't articulate anything specific about the company signals low effort or low interest. Interviewers want to see that you took the time to understand what the company actually does, what it values, and where it's headed.
  2. Are you a genuine fit? They want to know if your goals, values, and working style align with the team and the role. A great skills match means nothing if you'll be miserable in the culture or bored by the mission.
  3. Will you stick around? Hiring is expensive. If your answer suggests you're just looking for any paycheck, the interviewer sees a flight risk. They want someone who chose this company intentionally, not someone who applied to 200 jobs and landed here by accident.

Knowing this, you can build an answer that directly hits all three concerns.

The 3-Part Framework: Company Knowledge + Personal Connection + Mutual Value

Forget trying to be clever or memorizing a script. The strongest answers follow a simple, repeatable structure with three components.

1. Company Knowledge

Start by demonstrating that you know something specific and meaningful about the company. Not surface-level stuff like "you're a great company." Real details: a product you admire, a recent initiative, the company's approach to a problem in the industry, or something about the team culture that stood out to you.

This proves you did your research and you're not just going through the motions.

2. Personal Connection

Bridge the company detail to something in your own experience, values, or career goals. Why does what the company is doing matter to you specifically? What in your background makes you resonate with their mission or approach?

This is where you stop being generic and start being memorable. You're showing the interviewer that this isn't just another application for you.

3. Mutual Value

Close by explaining what you bring to the table and how it connects to what the company needs. You're not just here to take — you have skills, experience, and perspective that will genuinely help the team. Frame it as a two-way street: you want to contribute to what they're building, and you'll grow by doing it.

Pro Tip: Practice your answer out loud before the interview, but don't memorize it word-for-word. You want the structure internalized so you can deliver it naturally, like a conversation — not a rehearsed speech. Record yourself on your phone and listen back. If it sounds robotic, loosen it up. If it sounds vague, tighten the specifics.

How to Research a Company Before Your Interview

Your answer is only as good as your research. Here's where to look and what to look for, organized by how much time you have.

If You Have 15 Minutes

  • Read the company's careers or "About" page for mission and values statements.
  • Check their most recent blog posts or press releases for current initiatives.
  • Skim their LinkedIn page for recent company updates or milestones.

If You Have an Hour

  • Do everything above, plus:
  • Look up the company on Glassdoor or Indeed to understand culture and what employees say.
  • Search for recent news articles about the company — new products, expansions, funding rounds, leadership changes.
  • Check the interviewer's LinkedIn profile to understand their background and potential talking points.
  • Look at the company's social media to see what they're promoting and how they communicate.

If You Have a Day or More

  • Do everything above, plus:
  • Read the company's annual report or investor materials if publicly available.
  • Find talks or interviews given by company leaders.
  • Connect with current or former employees and ask what it's actually like to work there.
  • Use RoleCall to practice role-specific questions so you can tie your research directly to the job you're applying for.

The depth of your research should match the seniority of the role. A fast-food interview doesn't require investor reports. A director-level interview absolutely does.

5 Role-Specific Example Answers

Here's what this framework looks like in practice across five different career paths. Use these as starting points, then customize with your own details.

Software Engineer

"I've been following your platform's architecture decisions for a while — especially the move to event-driven microservices last year. That kind of technical ambition is exactly the kind of environment where I do my best work. At my last role, I led a similar migration that cut our response times by 40%, and I'm excited about the chance to tackle that kind of challenge at a larger scale with your team."

This answer works because it references a specific technical decision, connects it to personal experience with measurable results, and frames the contribution as scaling something they already care about.

Registered Nurse

"Your hospital's nurse residency program has a strong reputation — I've heard from several nurses who went through it that the mentorship structure is genuinely supportive, not just a box to check. I'm early in my nursing career, and that kind of investment in new nurses matters to me because I want to build real clinical confidence, not just survive my first year. I also noticed your unit's focus on patient education, which aligns with how I approach care — I believe informed patients recover better."

This one highlights a specific program, connects it to a career stage and values, and ties in a care philosophy. It tells the interviewer: this person chose us for a reason.

Teacher

"I read about your district's project-based learning initiative and watched the student showcase videos on your website. That's the kind of teaching I want to do — getting students to apply what they learn instead of just memorizing for tests. In my student teaching, I designed a cross-curricular project where students built a business plan using math, writing, and research skills, and the engagement was night and day compared to traditional lessons. I want to be somewhere that encourages that approach."

This demonstrates knowledge of the district's educational approach, provides a concrete teaching example, and frames the fit as shared philosophy — not just needing a job.

Marketing Manager

"I've been impressed by how your brand handled the product rebrand last quarter — the messaging pivot was smart and the social rollout felt cohesive, which is rare at that speed. I've led two rebrands in the past three years, and I know how hard it is to keep messaging tight across channels when things are moving fast. I'd love to bring that experience to your team, especially as you're scaling into new markets where brand consistency is going to matter even more."

This references a specific recent campaign, positions the candidate as someone who understands the challenge from experience, and looks forward to a future opportunity — not just the current state of things.

Electrician

"I know your shop does a lot of commercial work with energy-efficient retrofits, and that's the direction I want my career to go. I've been getting my additional certifications in solar and EV charging systems because I see the industry shifting, and I want to be on the crew that's doing that work, not watching from the sidelines. Your company is already there, and I want to contribute to and learn from that expertise."

This shows forward-thinking career planning, specific technical interest, and alignment with where the company is headed — not just where it is today. It signals ambition and initiative.

What NOT to Say

Knowing what to avoid is just as important as knowing what to include. Here are the answers that hurt your chances:

  • "I just need a job." Honest? Maybe. Helpful? Never. Even if it's true, this signals zero intentional interest in the company and makes you look like a flight risk.
  • "I heard you pay well." Compensation matters, but leading with it in an interview tells the interviewer you'll leave the moment someone offers more.
  • "You guys seem like a cool company." Vague flattery with no substance. If you can't replace "cool" with something specific, you haven't done enough research.
  • "I want to work here because I need experience." This frames the company as a stepping stone. Even if it's your first job, reframe it: you want to contribute while you learn, not just extract value.
  • Talking only about yourself. "I want to grow my skills" and "I want to advance my career" are fine motivations, but if your entire answer is about what the company can do for you, you've missed the point. Balance it with what you bring to them.
  • Being generic enough to copy-paste. If your answer works identically for any company in any industry, it's not specific enough. Swap the company name — if nothing else in your answer needs to change, start over.

Putting It All Together

Here's the mental checklist before you walk into the interview:

  1. You've researched the company — at least one specific detail you can reference naturally.
  2. You have a personal connection — why this company, this mission, or this approach resonates with your experience or values.
  3. You know your value — what you bring that connects to what they need right now.
  4. You've practiced out loud — enough to sound confident, not so much that you sound robotic.

The best answers to "Why do you want to work here?" don't feel like answers at all. They feel like a real person explaining a real decision. That's the goal. You're not performing — you're having a conversation about why this opportunity makes sense for both of you.
