What to Wear to a Job Interview (Every Industry)

Published May 2026 · RoleCall Interview Prep

You landed the interview. Now comes the question that trips up even seasoned professionals: what should I wear? The answer depends on the industry, the company culture, and whether you are meeting in person or over video. Getting it wrong can cost you the job before you even open your mouth. Getting it right signals professionalism, attention to detail, and cultural awareness.

This guide covers every major industry so you can walk in with confidence, no matter the role.

The Golden Rule: Dress One Level Up

When in doubt, dress one notch above what employees at the company typically wear. If the office is business casual, go business formal. If it is a jeans-and-tee startup, go smart casual with clean, polished pieces. The goal is to show respect for the opportunity while demonstrating that you understand the environment.

Research is critical here. Check the company website, LinkedIn employee photos, Glassdoor reviews, and social media. If you know someone who works there, ask them directly. There is no shame in it, and interviewers appreciate the effort to fit in.

Corporate and Business Formal

Industries like finance, law, consulting, and government tend to expect traditional business formal attire. This is the most conservative dress code and the one where mistakes are most noticed.

For Men

  • A well-fitted dark suit in navy, charcoal, or black
  • A crisp white or light blue dress shirt
  • A conservative tie with subtle patterns or solid colors
  • Polished leather dress shoes in black or dark brown
  • Dark socks that match the trousers
  • A leather belt that matches the shoe color
  • Minimal accessories: a classic watch, no flashy jewelry

For Women

  • A tailored suit with pants or a knee-length skirt
  • A conservative blouse in a complementary color
  • Closed-toe heels (2-3 inches) or polished flats
  • Neutral or sheer hosiery if wearing a skirt
  • Simple jewelry: stud earrings, a watch, one bracelet
  • A structured handbag or portfolio, not a casual tote

Business Casual

Business casual is the most common dress code across offices today, but it is also the most ambiguous. It generally means polished but not formal. Think chinos, button-downs, blazers without ties, and clean leather shoes.

For Men

  • Chinos or dress trousers in navy, gray, or khaki
  • A button-down shirt (tucked in) with or without a blazer
  • Leather shoes such as loafers, oxfords, or clean desert boots
  • A leather belt; no tie needed unless the role is senior-level

For Women

  • Blouse with dress pants, a midi skirt, or a sheath dress
  • A cardigan or blazer to add structure
  • Flats, loafers, or low heels in a neutral color
  • Professional accessories that complement without overpowering

Creative, Tech, and Startup Environments

Tech companies, design agencies, and startups generally have the most relaxed dress codes. However, showing up in sweatpants is still a bad idea. You want to look put-together without looking like you are trying too hard.

For a Software Engineer interview at a tech company, smart casual works well. Dark jeans or chinos with a clean, well-fitting shirt or sweater. Clean sneakers are acceptable in most tech environments, but avoid beat-up athletic shoes.

If you are interviewing for a Graphic Designer role, you have more freedom to express personal style. A clean, creative outfit that reflects your design sensibility can actually work in your favor. Think interesting textures, a statement piece, or a curated minimalist look.

Smart Casual Examples

  • Dark jeans (no rips) with a blazer and clean sneakers
  • Chinos with a fitted crew-neck sweater
  • A casual dress with ankle boots
  • Tailored pants with a tucked-in tee and a structured jacket

Healthcare and Medical Fields

Healthcare interviews are unique because many roles involve clinical settings. You will not be expected to wear scrubs to an interview, but you should look professional and practical.

For a Registered Nurse interview, business casual is the standard. A pressed blouse with dress pants or a simple dress with a cardigan is appropriate. Avoid anything too tight, too short, or with excessive jewelry that might seem impractical in a clinical setting.

Key Healthcare Dress Tips

  • Keep jewelry minimal and practical: small studs, a watch, one ring
  • Avoid strong perfume or cologne (many patients and staff are sensitive)
  • Closed-toe shoes are a must
  • Nails should be clean and trimmed; long acrylic nails are a red flag in clinical roles
  • If your hair is long, tie it back neatly

Skilled Trades and Construction

Interviews for electrician, plumber, welder, or construction supervisor roles often happen in office trailers or company headquarters. You want to look clean and capable, not like you just came from a job site, but also not like you are headed to a boardroom.

For an Electrician interview, think clean and practical. Ironed khakis or dark work pants, a collared polo or button-down work shirt, and clean boots or sturdy shoes. A clean company-branded shirt from a previous employer is fine if it is in good condition.

Trades Interview Outfit Tips

  • Clean, wrinkle-free collared shirt (polo or button-down)
  • Khaki or dark work pants, no jeans with stains or holes
  • Clean, polished work boots or sturdy shoes
  • A neat haircut and trimmed facial hair
  • Bring a folder with your resume, certifications, and references

Service Industry and Retail

For customer-facing roles in hospitality, retail, or food service, the goal is to look friendly, approachable, and professional. You want to show that you can represent the brand well.

  • Business casual works well for most service industry interviews
  • Pay attention to the brand aesthetic: if it is a luxury restaurant, dress more formally; if it is a casual cafe, smart casual is fine
  • Clean, polished shoes are important in roles where you will be on your feet
  • A warm smile and good posture matter as much as the clothes

Virtual and Video Interviews

Remote interviews have become the norm, and the rules are slightly different. You still need to dress professionally from at least the waist up, but you also need to think about your background, lighting, and camera angle.

Video Interview Setup

  • Dress fully professional even if you think the camera only shows your upper half. You never know when you might need to stand up.
  • Solid colors work best on camera. Avoid busy patterns, thin stripes, or pure white, which can cause visual distortion.
  • Test your camera and lighting. Sit facing a window or ring light. Avoid backlighting from windows behind you.
  • Clean up your background. A blank wall, bookshelf, or tidy office space works well. Blur or use a professional virtual background as a last resort.
  • Position the camera at eye level. Stack books under your laptop if needed.
  • Wear pants. This sounds obvious, but you might need to stand to grab something, and the camera could shift.

Pro Tip: Do a test video call with a friend 24 hours before your interview. Check your audio, video quality, internet stability, and how you look on screen. Fix any issues well before interview day.

Grooming and Hygiene Essentials

No matter what you wear, grooming can make or break your look. These basics apply across every industry and dress code.

Universal Grooming Checklist

  • Hair: Clean, styled, and out of your face. Get a haircut a few days before, not the day of.
  • Nails: Clean and trimmed. Clear or neutral polish if you wear it.
  • Breath: Brush, floss, and bring mints. Avoid gum during the interview.
  • Scent: Less is more. A light application of cologne or perfume is fine, but skip it entirely if you are unsure. Many offices are fragrance-free.
  • Skin: Moisturize your face and hands. Chapped lips or dry skin can be distracting.
  • Clothes: Iron or steam everything the night before. Wrinkles signal carelessness.
  • Shoes: Clean and polished. Interviewers notice shoes more than you think.

What NOT to Wear to an Interview

Some things are universally inappropriate for job interviews regardless of the industry.

  • Athletic wear: Joggers, hoodies, sneakers with athletic logos, gym clothes of any kind
  • Overly casual clothing: Flip-flops, graphic tees with slogans, ripped jeans, shorts
  • Too much skin: Low-cut tops, short skirts, tank tops, visible undergarments
  • Loud accessories: Oversized jewelry, novelty ties, hats (unless for religious reasons)
  • Strong fragrances: Can trigger allergies or headaches in small interview rooms
  • Wrinkled or stained clothing: Shows a lack of preparation and attention to detail
  • Visible brand logos: Large designer logos can come across as showy or careless
  • Last-minute outfit changes: Stick with something you have worn before and feel confident in

Pro Tip: Lay out your complete outfit the night before your interview, including shoes, accessories, and your bag. Try everything on together. Check for stains, missing buttons, and loose threads. Put a lint roller by the door.

Accessories and Final Touches

Accessories should complement your outfit, not compete with it. Here is what to consider.

The Right Bag

Bring a professional bag to carry your resume, portfolio, and essentials. A leather portfolio, structured tote, or clean messenger bag works well. Avoid backpacks unless you are in tech or academia, where they are more accepted.

Watch

A simple, classic watch is one of the best accessories for an interview. It signals punctuality and professionalism. Avoid smartwatches that light up or buzz during the conversation.

Jewelry

Keep it minimal. One watch, small earrings, one ring, and maybe a thin bracelet. If it jingles, rattles, or distracts, leave it at home.

Portfolio and Pen

Bring a notepad or portfolio with copies of your resume, a list of questions to ask, and a quality pen. This is practical and shows preparation.

The Bottom Line

Your outfit is the first thing an interviewer notices about you. It sets the tone before you say a word. The right clothing communicates that you respect the opportunity, understand the culture, and pay attention to detail. Spend 30 minutes researching the company dress code, lay out your outfit the night before, and focus your energy on nailing the actual interview questions.
