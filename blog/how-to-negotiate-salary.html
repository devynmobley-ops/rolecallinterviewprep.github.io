How to Negotiate Salary After a Job Offer

Published May 2026 · RoleCall Interview Prep

They offered you the job. Congratulations. But before you accept, take a breath: the offer is almost never the final number. According to multiple surveys, over 70% of employers expect candidates to negotiate, and the average salary negotiation results in a 7-10% increase. On a $60,000 offer, that is $4,200 to $6,000 more per year, compounding over your entire tenure.

Yet most people accept the first number without pushing back. Why? Fear. Fear of seeming ungrateful, fear of losing the offer, fear of awkwardness. This guide will give you the data, scripts, and confidence to negotiate effectively without burning bridges.

When to Negotiate (and When Not To)

The right time to negotiate salary is after you receive a written offer, not during initial screening calls and not after you have already accepted. The offer is your leverage point. The company has invested time and resources in interviewing you, and they want you to say yes.

Signs You Should Negotiate

  • You have a written offer in hand
  • Your research shows the offer is below market rate
  • You have competing offers or are currently employed
  • You bring specialized skills, certifications, or experience to the role
  • The role has been open for a while (they are motivated to close the hire)

When Negotiation May Not Be Wise

  • The offer is already at the top of the publicly posted salary band and you have no competing leverage
  • You are entry-level with minimal differentiation from other candidates (focus on other terms instead)
  • The company has explicitly stated the salary is non-negotiable (some government and union roles have fixed pay scales)

Important: Even when base salary is fixed, you can often negotiate signing bonuses, remote work flexibility, additional PTO, professional development budgets, or accelerated review timelines. Never assume negotiation is off the table entirely.

How to Research Your Market Rate

Negotiation without data is just guessing. You need to walk into the conversation with hard numbers about what your role, experience level, and location command in the market.

Best Salary Research Tools

  • Levels.fyi: Excellent for tech roles with verified compensation data by company and level
  • Glassdoor: Large database of self-reported salaries; cross-reference multiple entries
  • Payscale: Adjusts for location, experience, and education level
  • Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS): Government data on median wages by occupation and region
  • LinkedIn Salary: Aggregated data from LinkedIn members; useful for location-specific ranges
  • Blind (app): Anonymous professional community where employees share verified comp packages

How to Build Your Number

Collect data from at least three sources. Note the 25th percentile, median, and 75th percentile for your role in your metro area. Your target should fall between the median and 75th percentile. If you have 7+ years of experience, specialized certifications, or are in a high-demand field, aim for the 75th or above.

For example, if you are an Accountant with a CPA and five years of experience in a mid-size city, and the median is $75,000 with a 75th percentile of $88,000, your ask should be in the $82,000 to $88,000 range.

The Salary Negotiation Conversation: Step by Step

Most salary negotiations happen over the phone or email, rarely in person. Here is the exact flow.

Step 1: Express Gratitude and Enthusiasm

Start by thanking the hiring manager and reaffirming your interest. This sets a positive tone and makes it clear you are negotiating in good faith, not playing games.

Step 2: State Your Case with Data

Present your market research concisely. Do not ramble. You want a sentence or two that frames your ask as reasonable, not greedy.

Step 3: Make a Specific Ask

Give a specific number or range, not a vague request. Anchoring to a precise number (like $83,500 instead of $85,000) signals that you have done your homework.

Step 4: Pause and Listen

After you state your ask, stop talking. Let them respond. This is the hardest part, but filling the silence with nervous chatter weakens your position.

Step 5: Be Ready to Discuss

They will likely come back with a counteroffer, ask what is driving your number, or need time to check with their team. All of these are normal.

Negotiation Scripts You Can Use

Having the right words ready removes anxiety from the conversation. Adapt these scripts to your situation.

Script 1: The Standard Counter

"Thank you so much for the offer. I am thrilled about joining the team and contributing to [specific project or goal]. After reviewing the offer and researching market rates for this role with my experience level in [city], I was hoping we could discuss a base salary of $[your number]. Based on my research, this aligns with the market for someone with [your key qualification]. Is there flexibility to adjust the base?"

Script 2: With Competing Offers

"I appreciate the offer and I want to be transparent: I am also considering another opportunity at $[competing number]. Your role is my top choice because of [specific reason], and I would love to make this work. Is there room to bring the base closer to $[your number]?"

Script 3: When the Offer Is Significantly Below Market

"Thank you for extending the offer. I am very excited about this role. I want to be upfront: the base salary is lower than what my research shows for comparable positions in [city]. With my [X years of experience / certification / specific skill], I was expecting a range of $[X] to $[Y]. Could we explore options to close that gap?"

Script 4: Negotiating Non-Salary Terms

"I understand the base is firm. Given that, could we discuss other aspects of the package? I would particularly value [signing bonus / additional PTO / remote flexibility / professional development budget / earlier performance review]. These would make a meaningful difference for me."

Sales professionals and Sales Representatives often have the most practice with negotiation, but the principles apply to every role. HR professionals especially value candidates who approach the conversation respectfully and with data. If you are interviewing for an HR Specialist position, demonstrating negotiation savvy actually reinforces your qualifications.

Handling Counteroffers and Pushback

Not every negotiation goes smoothly. Here is how to handle common responses.

"This is our best and final offer."

Ask if they can confirm it is truly final, then evaluate the full package. If the base is locked, pivot to benefits, bonuses, or title. You can also ask for an earlier performance review with a salary adjustment: "Would you be open to a 90-day performance review with a salary adjustment to $[number] based on my contributions?"

"We need to check with leadership."

This is a good sign. They are not saying no. Ask for a timeline: "Of course. When do you expect to have an answer? I want to be respectful of both our timelines."

"We can offer $[slightly higher number]."

If it is close to your target, consider accepting. If there is still a gap, you can push one more time gently: "I appreciate the increase. Is there any flexibility to get to $[number]? That would make this an easy yes for me."

"What number do you need to say yes?"

This is a direct question and you should answer it directly. State your number confidently. Do not undersell yourself out of nervousness.

Pro Tip: Never accept or reject an offer on the phone in the moment. It is always appropriate to say: "Thank you. I would like to take 24-48 hours to review the full package. Can I get back to you by [specific day]?" This gives you time to think clearly.

Benefits Beyond Salary Worth Negotiating

Base salary is just one part of total compensation. These benefits have real monetary value and are often easier for employers to flex on.

  • Signing bonus: One-time payment to bridge a gap. Common when base salary is fixed.
  • Annual bonus: Negotiate the target percentage or guaranteed first-year minimum.
  • Equity/Stock options: Negotiate the number of shares, vesting schedule, and strike price.
  • Remote work: Full or partial remote can save you $5,000-$15,000 per year in commuting costs.
  • Additional PTO: Even 2-3 extra days per year adds up over a career.
  • Professional development: Conference budgets, certification reimbursement, tuition assistance.
  • Relocation assistance: Moving costs, temporary housing, travel for house-hunting trips.
  • Performance review timeline: A 6-month review instead of 12-month can accelerate your first raise.
  • Title: A better title has long-term career value, even if the pay is the same.

Common Salary Negotiation Mistakes

Avoid these errors that can cost you money or the offer entirely.

  • Giving a number too early: When asked for salary expectations in screening, deflect with: "I would like to learn more about the role and responsibilities before discussing specific numbers. Can you share the budgeted range for this position?"
  • Lying about competing offers: Companies sometimes ask for proof. If you fabricate offers and get caught, the offer will be rescinded.
  • Negotiating via text or chat: Salary conversations deserve the formality of a phone call or email.
  • Making it personal: Do not cite personal expenses (rent, student loans, childcare) as justification. Focus on market data and your value.
  • Apologizing for negotiating: Never say "I am sorry to ask, but..." or "I feel bad bringing this up." You are not doing anything wrong.
  • Accepting verbally before getting it in writing: Always get the final offer with agreed-upon terms in writing before accepting.

The Bottom Line

Salary negotiation is not confrontational. It is a normal part of the hiring process that employers expect and respect. The worst outcome of a reasonable negotiation is that they say no and you still have the original offer. The best outcome is thousands of additional dollars per year for as long as you stay in the role.
