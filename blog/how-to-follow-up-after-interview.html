How to Follow Up After an Interview (With Templates)

Published May 2026 · RoleCall Interview Prep

The interview went well. You shook hands, exchanged smiles, and walked out feeling confident. Now what? Many candidates drop the ball right at the finish line by failing to follow up, or by following up poorly. A thoughtful follow-up can be the difference between getting the offer and being the runner-up.

Following up is not about nagging or desperation. It is about demonstrating professionalism, reinforcing your interest, and staying top of mind in a process where hiring managers are evaluating multiple candidates simultaneously. This guide covers exactly when, how, and what to say.

Why Follow-Up Matters More Than You Think

Hiring managers often interview several candidates over a period of days or weeks. Without a follow-up, you become a name on a list. A well-crafted thank-you email does three things:

  • It refreshes their memory. Reference a specific topic from your conversation and you instantly stand out from candidates who sent generic or no follow-up.
  • It demonstrates soft skills. Timeliness, attention to detail, and communication ability are all reflected in your follow-up. These are exactly the traits employers value.
  • It reopens the conversation. A follow-up gives you the chance to address anything you wish you had said differently, add a relevant detail, or share a resource.

Studies consistently show that fewer than half of candidates send a thank-you note after an interview. Simply sending one puts you ahead of the majority.

The Timing Rules

Timing matters as much as content. Follow these guidelines.

Thank-You Email: Within 24 Hours

Send your thank-you email the same day or the next morning. For morning interviews, send by end of day. For afternoon interviews, send by the following morning at the latest. Beyond 24 hours, the impact diminishes rapidly.

Post-Interview Check-In: After the Stated Timeline Has Passed

If the interviewer said you would hear back in a week, wait until day 8 or 9. If they said two weeks, wait until day 15. Following up before their stated timeline is premature and can come across as impatient.

Second Follow-Up: 7-10 Days After the First

If you have not heard back after your initial check-in, one more follow-up a week to ten days later is appropriate. After that, move on. Two follow-ups is the professional maximum. Continued contact after that borders on harassment.

Pro Tip: If the interviewer gave you a specific date for a decision and that date passes with no word, you have a legitimate reason to follow up. Do not feel guilty about it. Hiring timelines slip all the time, and a polite nudge is perfectly appropriate.

Thank-You Email Templates

Adapt these templates to your situation. Personalization is critical. A generic thank-you note is barely better than sending nothing at all. Reference specific topics, people, or details from your conversation.

Template 1: The Standard Thank-You (After First Interview)

Subject: Thank you - [Role Title] interview

Hi [Interviewer Name],

Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the [Role Title] position. I enjoyed learning more about [specific topic you discussed, e.g., "the team's approach to customer onboarding" or "the upcoming product roadmap"].

Our conversation reinforced my excitement about this opportunity. I believe my experience in [relevant skill/experience] would allow me to contribute meaningfully to [specific goal or challenge they mentioned].

Please let me know if there is any additional information I can provide. I look forward to hearing about next steps.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Template 2: Panel Interview Thank-You

When you interview with multiple people, send individual emails to each person. Reference something unique from your conversation with each interviewer so the messages do not look like a mass send.

Hi [Interviewer Name],

Thank you for meeting with me today. I especially appreciated your perspective on [something specific they said or asked about]. It gave me a clearer picture of how the [department/team] operates and where I could add value.

The discussion about [specific topic] was particularly interesting to me because [brief connection to your experience]. I am confident my background in [skill] would help the team [specific outcome].

Thanks again for your time, and I look forward to the possibility of working together.

Best,
[Your Name]

Template 3: Thank-You With a Follow-Up Thought

Use this when you left the interview and immediately thought of a better answer to a question, or want to share something relevant you forgot to mention.

Hi [Interviewer Name],

Thank you for the engaging conversation today about the [Role Title] position. I wanted to follow up on your question about [topic]. After thinking more about it, I wanted to add that [your improved answer or additional context].

I also came across [an article / a case study / a resource] related to [topic you discussed] and thought you might find it interesting: [link or brief description].

I remain very enthusiastic about this role and the opportunity to contribute to [company goal]. Looking forward to next steps.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Template 4: Checking In After the Decision Timeline Has Passed

Hi [Interviewer Name],

I hope you are doing well. I wanted to check in regarding the [Role Title] position. I understand these processes can take time, and I appreciate the thoroughness of your team's approach.

I remain very interested in the role and would love to know if there are any updates on the timeline or if there is any additional information I can provide to help with the decision.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Template 5: Second Follow-Up (Final Attempt)

Hi [Interviewer Name],

I wanted to reach out one more time regarding the [Role Title] position. I understand that hiring decisions involve many factors, and I respect whatever direction you decide to take.

If the position has been filled, I would genuinely appreciate any feedback you might be willing to share about my interview. If there are future opportunities that might be a fit, I would welcome the chance to be considered.

Thank you again for your time and the thoughtful conversation we had.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Roles that require meticulous communication skills, like a Paralegal or Executive Assistant, benefit especially from polished follow-up. Your email writing ability is literally being evaluated. An Marketing Manager candidate should use the follow-up to demonstrate the same persuasive communication skills the role demands.

What to Say If You Have Not Heard Back

Silence after an interview is frustrating but normal. Hiring processes are messy. Budgets get frozen, priorities shift, decision-makers go on vacation, and roles get restructured. Here is how to handle it.

After One Week of Silence

If the stated timeline was one week, send your first check-in (Template 4 above). Keep it brief, professional, and positive. Do not express frustration.

After Two Weeks of Silence

Send your second follow-up (Template 5). This is your last proactive outreach. After this, the ball is in their court.

After Three or More Weeks

Assume the role has been filled or put on hold. Continue your job search. If they eventually come back with an offer, great. If not, you have not wasted weeks waiting. Sometimes companies do come back months later when a similar role opens up, and your professional follow-up will be remembered.

Pro Tip: If you are working with a recruiter, route your follow-ups through them rather than going directly to the hiring manager. The recruiter can give you honest feedback on where things stand and whether additional follow-up would help or hurt.

How Many Times Should You Follow Up?

The answer is simple: two follow-ups maximum after the initial thank-you email. Here is the full timeline:

  1. Day 0-1: Send the thank-you email (Templates 1-3)
  2. After stated timeline + 2 days: Send check-in (Template 4)
  3. 7-10 days later: Send final follow-up (Template 5)
  4. After that: Move on. They have your information and know how to reach you.

If the company is interested, they will contact you. No amount of follow-up will change a no into a yes. What follow-up can do is tip a close decision in your favor and ensure you are not overlooked due to administrative delays.

What NOT to Do When Following Up

Following up poorly is worse than not following up at all. Avoid these mistakes.

  • Do not follow up too soon. Sending a check-in before the stated decision timeline shows impatience and poor judgment.
  • Do not follow up too often. More than two follow-ups after your thank-you email crosses the line from persistent to pushy.
  • Do not use guilt or pressure. Avoid phrases like "I really need this job" or "I have other offers expiring." Even if true, it comes across as manipulative.
  • Do not contact multiple people on the team. Send your follow-up to your primary interviewer or recruiter. Emailing the CEO, the hiring manager, and the HR rep separately looks desperate.
  • Do not follow up on personal channels. Do not send messages on personal social media, call a cell phone number you found online, or show up at the office. Use professional channels only.
  • Do not write novels. Your thank-you email should be 100-150 words. Your check-in should be even shorter. Respect the reader's time.
  • Do not forget to proofread. A follow-up email with typos, the wrong company name, or the wrong interviewer name is worse than no email at all. Read it out loud before sending.
  • Do not attach files unless asked. Do not attach your portfolio, writing samples, or additional references unless the interviewer requested them.

Special Considerations by Interview Type

Phone Screens and Initial Interviews

A brief, warm thank-you is appropriate. Keep it to 3-4 sentences. You are reinforcing interest, not writing an essay.

Final Round Interviews

Put more effort into your follow-up. Reference specific conversations, reiterate your strongest qualifications for the role, and express genuine enthusiasm. This is often the email that gets shared with the broader decision-making team.

Informational Interviews

Thank the person for their time and insights. Mention one specific piece of advice they shared and how you plan to apply it. This builds a lasting professional relationship regardless of whether a job results.

The Bottom Line

Following up after an interview is not optional. It is a core part of the job search process that demonstrates professionalism, communication skills, and genuine interest. Send your thank-you within 24 hours, check in once after the stated timeline, and send one final follow-up if needed. Keep every message brief, personalized, and professional.

The candidates who get the offer are rarely the ones who interviewed best. They are the ones who did everything well, including the follow-up.
